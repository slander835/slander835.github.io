About

Welcome! I am a PhD student at the University of Texas at Austin's Department of Government. My research sits at the intersection betweeen international political economy and comparative industrial policy. After completing my Bachelor of Arts in Political Science from Columbia University in 2025, I started my PhD at UT under the academic advisement of Nate Jensen and Rachel Wellhausen.

Research Interests

I'm interested in studying how governments and firms work together to provide public goods. Specifically, I seek to understand government risk management when engaging with private partners in public-private partnerships, and government incentives to encourage technological innovation.

Current Projects

Political Determinants of Risk Sharing in Public-Private Partnerships
2026 - Present

What explains the variation in risk sharing mechanisms in public-private partnerships (PPPs)? PPPs are structured contractual agreements between a government and a private partner to produce a public good. A defining feature of PPPs is the transfer of risk from the government to the private partner, which implies that governments seek to minize potential failures of public investment projects, assumingly to secure reelection. Yet, different contract designs yield different risk allocations. This paper posits that electoral accountability plays a role in the risk-sharing contract design, but also theorizes that the private partner anticipating electoral suspectibility may shift risk in their favor.

Innovative Winners: Variation of Technological Innovation & Fitness in Industrial Policy
2025 - Present

What explains the variation in technological innovation in public-private partnerships? This paper investigates the role that governments may play as strategic signalers altering the variation of products in the marketplace. More specifically, I argue that markets can be persuaded by signals from the government. I propose that market dom- inance may not be solely determined by intrinsic product qualities, like innovative-ness, but that governments can strategically signal to markets about product fitness, favor- ing goods that meet operational requirements independently of their inherent technical merit. To flesh out these mechanisms, I develop a multi-stage signaling model in which governments signal to markets on two dimensions: product fitness and innovative-ness. Equilibrium analysis reveals that firm research and development allocation is not only responsive to government signaling but is adaptive to future anticipated government needs. The model is complemented by field work interviews conducted in Taiwan, which illustrate the differences in innovative outcomes between Taiwan's semicondcutor and biotechnology industries.
